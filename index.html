Overview

In this project, I explored how perspective, focal length, and camera positioning affect images by capturing and comparing different photo setups. I experimented with portraits to see distortion from close-up selfies versus zoomed shots, studied architectural perspective compression by shooting urban scenes at different zooms, and recreated the classic dolly zoom effect through a sequence of photos compiled into an animated GIF. Through these exercises, I built an intuitive understanding of how camera distance and zoom interact to shape visual perception.

1. Selfie: The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

As I moved from super close up to farther back, the proportions of my friendâ€™s face changed. The close-up shot exaggerated certain features, like making the nose look bigger, while stepping back and zooming in made everything look more natural and balanced. This happens because in photography the depth, or z-dimension, gets flattened, so being too close creates distortion while distance and zoom help restore realistic proportions.

2. Architectural Perspective Compression

When I zoomed in from far away, the buildings and people in the distance looked much closer to the ones in the foreground, almost as if the whole street had been compressed together. But when I walked forward and took the shot without zoom, the depth came back. You can see the spacing between people and objects much more clearly. This happens because zoom in a way "compresses" perspective, while moving physically closer lets the photo capture depth much better (still not better than our eyes, though).

3. The Dolly Zoom

For this part, I used the Pappy Waldorf statue as my subject to create the dolly zoom effect. By stepping back while zooming in, I kept the statue roughly the same size in each frame, but the background shifted dramatically. It feels like the space around the statue is stretching and compressing because the change in focal length alters perspective while the subject remains constant.
