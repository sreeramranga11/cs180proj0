CS 180: Computer Vision Project 0

Sreeram Ranga

Overview

In this project, I explored how perspective, focal length, and camera positioning affect images by capturing and comparing different photo setups. I experimented with portraits to see distortion from close-up selfies versus zoomed shots, studied architectural perspective compression by shooting urban scenes at different zooms, and recreated the classic dolly zoom effect through a sequence of photos compiled into an animated GIF. Through these exercises, I built an intuitive understanding of how camera distance and zoom interact to shape visual perception.

1. Selfie: The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

As I went from super close up to farther back, the proportions of the face changed. The close-up shot exaggerated and stretched certain features, while stepping back and zooming in smoothed everything out, making the face look more natural. This happens because in photography, the z-dimension, or depth, is essentially flattened, so distance and zoom play a big role in how realistic the final image looks.

2. Architectural Perspective Compression

When I zoomed in from far away, the street looked flatter, with the buildings and objects in the distance appearing closer together than they really are. But when I walked forward and took the shot without zoom, the sense of depth came back. You can see the spacing between objects much more clearly.

3. The Dolly Zoom

For this part, I used the Pappy Waldorf statue as my subject to create the dolly zoom effect. By stepping back while zooming in, I kept the statue roughly the same size in each frame, but the background shifted dramatically. It feels like the space around the statue is stretching and compressing. Combining the shots into a GIF made the effect really pop.
